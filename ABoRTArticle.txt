The ABoRT Act: Reclaiming True Ownership of Your Vehicle—and Ensuring Your Right to Use it

The relentless grind of a Tucson commute has a way of stealing pieces of your soul. For years I drove my Jeep Wrangler, and many other vehicles, from the northwest to the east side, wondering if my 25-minute trip turning into an hour was just bad luck, or something more. Red light after red light. Barely moving. It seems like it was intentional. Friends balked at the suggestion: “That’s traffic, deal with it!” But the pattern was too consistent to be random. When I finally researched it, the answer was not merely incompetence or bad luck—it was design.
Tucson (like many cities) has adopted “traffic calming” measures and “road diets”: narrower lanes, added bike infrastructure, signal timing deliberately set to slow progression speeds. The stated goal is safety—fewer high-speed crashes, more people comfortable walking or biking. That goal matters. But when the primary effect is manufactured congestion that punishes drivers during rush hour—while safer, smarter alternatives (adaptive signals, better coordination, real capacity, pedestrian underpasses) sit on the shelf—it stops feeling like safety and starts feeling like coercion. You are not just delayed; you are being taught a lesson: slow down, change modes, or suffer.
The roads themselves further catalyze the sense of burning frustration. Potholes and uneven pavement here are not occasional; they are chronic. Driving that a Wrangler on those roads turns routine errands into a mechanical bull ride—every crater slamming the suspension, every patch threatening a blowout. I have seen smoother highways abroad, roads engineered for decades of abuse rather than quick fixes that reopen after the next monsoon. Why do we accept “cheap now, expensive later” maintenance that shifts the real cost onto drivers through blown tires, alignment bills, lost time, and real danger? Because no one is forced to answer for the neglect.
Then there is the vehicle you’re forced to trust with your life and wallet. I chose a mechanically simple Wrangler because ownership should mean control—fix it yourself, keep it running for decades, no middleman required. Newer vehicles increasingly betray that promise. Proprietary telematics lock basic features behind subscriptions. Parts “pairing” means only dealers can swap modules. Firmware can remotely limit or disable functions post-sale. And the price tag? Average new-vehicle transaction prices have climbed to record territory—hovering near or crossing $50,000 in recent months—pushed higher by layers of mandated complexity that price out working families and young adults.
The Toyota Hilux—legendary for being unbreakable in deserts, wars, and farms worldwide—remains largely unavailable new in the U.S. because of the same thorny regulatory thicket: safety standards, emissions rules, and compliance costs that favor expensive, software-heavy designs over simple, durable ones. Yet once a Hilux hits 25 years old, import exemptions kick in and suddenly it’s legal again. Why should Americans have to wait a quarter-century for access to the kind of reliable, owner-maintainable truck the rest of the world takes for granted?
These aren’t separate frustrations. They are symptoms of the same quiet theft: mobility being slowly converted from a right into a managed privilege.
Manufacturers enclose your car behind digital fences—subscription gates, locked diagnostics, remote capability subtraction. Governments tolerate crumbling roads, engineer gridlock to force “mode shift,” and allow transit to become unpredictable or unsafe. Together they erode something fundamental: the ability to move freely, own what you buy, and live without asking permission from a vendor’s software stack or a city’s dysfunction.
That erosion has a name in law: an undue burden on the right to travel—a right the Supreme Court has repeatedly called fundamental, protected by the Constitution itself (Crandall v. Nevada, Shapiro v. Thompson, Saenz v. Roe). When modern threats—digital enclosure, forced obsolescence, mobility-based surveillance, intentional throughput degradation, chronic infrastructure neglect, unsafe transit—chip away at practical exercise of that right, we stop being free in the ways that matter most.
That’s why I wrote the Automotive Bill of Rights for Transportation Act (ABoRT).

This is not a rejection of safety or progress. It is a demand for both—without sacrificing freedom, ownership, or efficiency.

The Core Problem and Why It Matters

Mobility in America is quietly getting fenced in from two directions. Private control over the machines people rely upon, and public policy that increasingly treats movement as something to manage instead of something to respect. Most people feel it first as irritation from traffic that never flows, roads that never stay fixed, and vehicles that keep getting more expensive and harder to fix. However, beneath all that grating and scraping against our patience is a real shift in power away from citizens and owners, toward institutions and vendors.
Start with the car itself. A modern vehicle can behave less like property and more like a tightly controlled service. Telematics and “connected” systems create subscription paywalls for features that feel basic. Parts pairing and locked diagnostics make routine repairs dependent on dealerships and proprietary tools, preventing people from buying cheap eBay parts and scoring cheap used parts at a local junkyard. Worse yet, software control enables remote subtraction of capability—Tesla has drawn attention for remotely disabling driver-assistance features on a used vehicle after purchase when the company asserted the buyer “did not pay” for them. Whether or not a company thinks it has a contractual justification, the practical message to ordinary owners is the same: you don’t really own your car. That is digital enclosure—property that can be diminished at a distance, meaning that you cannot buy a product and trust that it will remain as it was when it was purchased.
Now add the affordability squeeze. Average new-vehicle transaction prices have climbed to record territory—recently hovering near or crossing the $50,000 mark—which pushes basic automotive mobility out of reach for working families and young adults trying to become independent. When rules and market incentives consistently reward complexity—more sensors, more modules, more vendor-locked systems—simple, durable, repairable vehicles (the “Hilux/Wrangler class”) become harder to buy and harder to maintain. Even the popularity of the Toyota Hilux as a symbol matters here: globally it represents rugged simplicity, but in the U.S. market, trade barriers like the “Chicken Tax” and other economic/compliance factors help shape what manufacturers choose to sell. Regardless of the exact mix of causes, Americans can feel the outcome: fewer affordable, durable options—and more “subscription on wheels” design.
Then there is the government side of the vise. In too many cities, infrastructure policy tolerates chronic disrepair and treats congestion as a tool. Potholes become permanent features, patched poorly, then reopened by heat and rain. Meanwhile, certain “road diet” and traffic-calming projects explicitly trade vehicle throughput for other goals, and Tucson’s 5th/6th Street corridor analysis shows the city actively studying reconfiguration impacts on operations and travel time—proof that delay can be designed, not merely suffered. A free society should not require people to accept engineered inefficiency as penance for driving, especially when better engineering can solve safety and flow together.
These trends also revive an older constitutional concern: the right to travel. The Supreme Court has treated travel as a fundamental component of national citizenship and constitutional structure. In Saenz v. Roe, the Court described three core components of the right to travel: to enter and leave states, to be treated as a welcome visitor, and—after moving—to be treated like other citizens of the new state. When travel becomes burdened through coercive design, surveillance-by-mobility, routine checkpoints, or policy choices that effectively confine people by cost and dysfunction, the right becomes theoretical instead of real.
Finally, public transit must serve families and workers—not function as a predictable venue for intimidation, open drug use, or easy crime displacement. A system that silently tolerates disorder does not just inconvenience riders—it quietly erodes trust, pushes families away, and becomes a self-fulfilling failure.
The point is not nostalgia for what once was. Rather, the point is freedom in the practical sense: the ability to move, work, and live without asking permission from a vendor’s software stack or a city’s congestion experiment. The ABoRT Act exists to draw that line clearly—ownership means control, infrastructure means durability, and mobility means liberty.
Introducing the ABoRT Act (Automotive Bill of Rights for Transportation Act)

The Automotive Bill of Rights for Transportation Act (ABoRT Act) treats mobility the way a free country should: as a civil liberty with enforceable boundaries, not a privilege rationed by planners, vendors, and “smart” systems that quietly shift power away from ordinary people. It is not “more regulation for the sake of regulation.” It is a liberty-focused framework that limits coercion and capture while demanding competence, durability, and honest ownership. The goal is simple: if people lawfully own a vehicle and lawfully use public infrastructure, neither corporations nor governments should be able to corral them through digital lockouts, engineered dysfunction, or surveillance-by-default.

ABoRT is comprehensive because the problem is comprehensive. The car is increasingly controlled by software and service contracts, while cities and states increasingly treat congestion, neglect, and transit disorder as acceptable collateral to force behavior. You cannot fix that with a single narrow reform. A right-to-repair bill alone will not stop traffic shaping. A road funding bill alone will not stop subscription hostage tactics. A privacy bill alone will not stop remote disablement. ABoRT pulls these threads together into one coherent promise: mobility remains practical, affordable, and free.

The Act rests on a clear constitutional posture. Its Findings ground the bill in the Supreme Court’s right-to-travel doctrine, including the idea that travel is a fundamental liberty tied to national citizenship and the structure of union—then translates that doctrine into modern conditions, where the obstacles are no longer just explicit residency penalties, but digital enclosure, mobility surveillance, and coercive infrastructure design. That is the point of the Findings: to make clear that the Act protects a constitutional right from updated forms of burden and deterrence, not from imaginary threats.

Key pillars of the ABoRT Act:
	1.	Travel and mobility as civil rights, not conditional privileges.
ABoRT codifies travel as a civil liberty in practical terms. It prohibits routine checkpoints and internal-passport behavior absent reasonable suspicion. It also bars “confinement-by-design”—policies that functionally penalize movement through engineered barriers, algorithmic scoring, or movement conditioning schemes. The message is that government may pursue safety, but it may not do so by converting normal travel into a managed permission system.
	2.	A real right to repair—meaningful, not symbolic.
ABoRT requires manufacturers to provide owners and independent repair providers access to parts, tools, diagnostics, documentation, and calibration pathways on fair terms. It prohibits repair-blocking tactics such as DRM lockouts, parts pairing, service gating, and firmware restrictions that exist primarily to force dealership dependence. It establishes warranty fairness so companies cannot void coverage merely because an owner used an independent shop. And it adds a crucial preservation rule: when manufacturers discontinue essential parts or support, owners and independent providers may lawfully replicate or reverse-engineer compatible parts for repair and maintenance, provided safety standards are met and trademarks are not used deceptively. That one clause keeps classics, off-roaders, and utility vehicles from being “soft-banned” through scarcity.
	3.	No forced obsolescence, subscription hostage tactics, or digital sabotage.
ABoRT rejects the idea of “subscriptions on wheels” for essential function. If a feature is essential to ordinary use, companies cannot hold it hostage behind ongoing payments, account dependence, or remote policy changes that subtract capability after purchase. Updates may address real safety defects, but the Act blocks pretextual downgrades and prohibits remote degradation as a business model.
	4.	No mobility-based mass surveillance or social scoring.
Transportation systems generate extraordinarily sensitive data—where people go, when, how often, and with whom. ABoRT prohibits using mobility infrastructure as a channel for mass surveillance or behavioral scoring. It restricts retention and requires meaningful legal process for intrusive collection. The core principle is that the right to travel becomes hollow if the price of movement is constant tracking.
	5.	Durable roads with measurable accountability—and no coercive “traffic shaping.”
ABoRT imposes an enforceable duty to maintain and modernize roads to durable standards, backed by public dashboards that make failure visible: pothole density, time-to-repair, resurfacing cycles, and independent audits. It forces cure plans when jurisdictions miss standards, and it ties accountability to funding so neglect cannot hide behind bureaucracy. It also bans intentional throughput degradation used as coercion—“traffic shaping” whose primary purpose is to manufacture congestion, punish driving, or force mode shift. If a jurisdiction claims safety, it must prove necessity and use the least liberty-restrictive means.
	6.	Capacity must expand when congestion becomes chronic.
When delay and unreliability exceed defined thresholds over sustained time, ABoRT requires real capacity solutions—bypasses, beltways, connectors, grade separations, or equivalents—unless a jurisdiction proves physical infeasibility or disproportionate harm and adopts an alternative that achieves comparable throughput. This prevents the slow-motion trap where cities grow, corridors fail, and leadership simply tells residents to accept dysfunction.
	7.	Safe, self-funding transit that does not enable predictable crime.
ABoRT requires transit systems to maintain orderly, family-safe environments, with proportionate enforcement and due process for exclusions. It rejects the idea that “public” means “lawless.” Transit cannot function as a predictable venue for drug use, intimidation, fencing stolen goods, or routine victimization. The Act also pushes transit toward self-funding via fares and lawful revenue, with narrow, explicit hardship and disability subsidies that are transparent and audited.
	8.	Strong enforcement that ordinary people can use.
Rights without remedies are slogans. ABoRT includes a private right of action, fee shifting for prevailing plaintiffs, robust injunctive relief, and “no immunity” language so violations do not disappear behind technical shields. If a city neglects roads for years, if a vendor blocks repair through lockouts, or if a transit system tolerates predictable disorder, people can sue and win meaningful relief.

Finally, the Act carries a cultural and economic safeguard: the Hilux/Wrangler Standard. It preserves access to a class of simple, durable, owner-maintainable vehicles by prohibiting rules that quietly regulate them out of existence through cost, complexity, connectivity mandates, and vendor-controlled dependencies. This is not anti-safety. It is pro-reality: a free society must keep basic mobility within reach, especially for workers, rural residents, tradespeople, and families who cannot absorb $50,000 vehicles and dealership-only repair ecosystems.

ABoRT is drafted as a standalone federal model with preemption where necessary to stop lock-in and coercion, but it is also designed to be adapted. An Arizona version can directly target the problems people see every day—signal timing abuse, pothole neglect, and transit disorder—while preserving the same rights architecture. In short: ABoRT does not ask permission for freedom. It defines freedom, draws lines around it, and gives citizens the tools to enforce it.

Why Now?

The pressure on ordinary mobility is no longer theoretical. It strikes people in their wallets, their commutes, their patience, and their independence—right now.
New vehicles have become so expensive—averaging near or above $50,000 in recent months—that they may as well be considered a luxury good. Beneath the price tag lies a deeper transformation: the machines themselves grow more complex, more software-dependent, and far harder to repair without proprietary tools and dealer authorization. Features that once belonged unequivocally to the buyer have become leased privileges: account-linked functions, subscription gates, parts pairing, locked diagnostics, and telematics systems that turn the vehicle into a rolling data source. A person can still hold the title to the car, but the reality increasingly resembles a permissioned service. The normalization of enclosure and control—modern, digital, and quiet—is yet another failure of the system, but one we can correct.
At the same time, infrastructure degrades in ways impossible to ignore. Potholes, patchwork repairs, and deferred maintenance shift real costs onto drivers through blown tires, suspension damage, lost time, and genuine safety risk. Congestion worsens, and too often it is treated as something to “manage” rather than solve—whether through neglect, bureaucratic paralysis, or policies that tolerate delay as a behavior-shaping tool. When a city designs or maintains streets in a way that makes normal commutes miserable, the result is not mere inconvenience. It is a practical burden on the right to travel and a hidden tax on working people’s lives. It drains them emotionally and financially.
Public transit debates sharpen the urgency further. A system that is unsafe or disorderly does not serve the public; it drives families and workers away and becomes a predictable venue for crime and horror. Yet the answer cannot be coercion. People should choose transit because it is genuinely safe, functional, and economical—not because roads are deliberately engineered to fail or because vehicle ownership has been priced and locked out of reach.
Some reform efforts already exist—right-to-repair proposals are gaining momentum, and that matters. But ABoRT goes further because the problem is larger. Repair rights mean little if vehicles can be remotely degraded, if affordable durable classes are regulated out of existence, if roads remain chronically neglected, and if mobility becomes a surveillance channel. ABoRT sets the baseline for what a free people should be able to expect: true ownership, practical repairability, durable infrastructure, efficient flow, safe transit, and travel without routine suspicion.
Personal motivation drives this work. The direction is anti-liberty, inequitable, and unnecessary. A person should be able to choose a vehicle, maintain it independently, travel to work on roads that are competently maintained, and live without movement being shaped by coercion, dysfunction, or digital control. The faster the line gets drawn, the faster daily life stops feeling like a slow squeeze.
What to Do Next?

ABoRT is written as a model bill on purpose.
It is not meant to gather dust in a PDF graveyard.
It is meant to be copied, argued over, adapted, improved, and pushed into real policy—federal or state—wherever people feel the squeeze of crumbling roads, engineered congestion, unsafe transit, and vehicles that increasingly behave like rented software.
The practical path forward looks the same as any serious open-source project:
Read it.
Fork it.
Propose changes.
Start building local coalitions around the parts that solve local pain.

A person does not need to agree with every line to help.
The point is to establish a liberty-first baseline and force the conversation back to ownership, durability, safety, and competence.
If Tucson has taught anything, it is that “just accept it” becomes an excuse for everything: worse commutes, worse roads, worse transit, and more expensive vehicles with less control.
That is not inevitable.
ABoRT exists so citizens can stop arguing in the abstract and start pointing to a specific standard:
mobility stays practical,
repair stays lawful,
infrastructure stays competent,
and transit stays safe enough for families.
Mobility is not a privilege to be rationed.
It is a right with real-world meaning: the ability to move, work, build, and live without asking permission from a vendor’s software stack or a city’s dysfunction.
If ABoRT helps draw that line, then it is doing its job.
