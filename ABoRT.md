
## Automotive Bill of Rights for Transportation Act (ABoRT Act)
A BILL
To secure the right to travel; to protect ownership, repair, and control of lawfully owned vehicles and conveyances; to require durable, modernized, and efficient transportation infrastructure; to ensure safe and self-funding public transit; to prohibit mobility-based surveillance and coercion; and for other purposes.

## SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the “Automotive Bill of Rights for Transportation Act” or the “ABoRT Act.”

## SECTION. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds the following:

(1) The right to travel freely throughout the United States is a fundamental civil liberty inherent in national citizenship and protected by the Constitution, including the Privileges or Immunities Clause, the Privileges and Immunities Clause of Article IV, the Due Process Clause, and the Equal Protection Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment, as well as the structural principles of federal union.

(2) The Supreme Court has long recognized this right as fundamental. In Crandall v. Nevada, 73 U.S. 35 (1868), the Court held that the right to travel interstate is an essential attribute of national citizenship that cannot be unduly burdened by state action.

(3) In Shapiro v. Thompson, 394 U.S. 618 (1969), the Court affirmed that the right to interstate travel is a fundamental freedom, and any classification or penalty that burdens or deters its exercise must survive strict scrutiny, as it cannot be justified by mere administrative convenience or deterrence of migration.

(4) In Saenz v. Roe, 526 U.S. 489 (1999), the Court clarified that the constitutional right to travel encompasses at least three components:

(A) the right of a citizen of one State to enter and to leave another State;

(B) the right to be treated as a welcome visitor rather than an unfriendly alien when temporarily present in another State; and

(C) for those who elect to become permanent residents, the right to be treated like other citizens of that State.

(5) These protections extend to preventing unreasonable burdens, penalties, or restrictions on free movement, including those imposed through transportation policy, infrastructure neglect, surveillance, or coercive design that effectively confine individuals or convert travel from a right into a conditional privilege.

(6) Modern threats to this fundamental right—including digital enclosure of vehicles, forced obsolescence, mobility-based mass surveillance, intentional throughput degradation (traffic shaping), chronic infrastructure disrepair, and public transit environments that enable criminal conduct—undermine the practical exercise of travel liberties and impose undue burdens on interstate and intrastate movement, commerce, and personal freedom.

(7) This Act is necessary to safeguard the right to travel by ensuring nondiscriminatory access to safe, efficient, and owner-controlled modes of conveyance; prohibiting pretextual restrictions or coercion through transportation systems; and enforcing duties on jurisdictions to maintain essential mobility infrastructure, consistent with Congress's authority under the Commerce Clause, the Spending Clause, and the enforcement provisions of the Fourteenth Amendment.

## SECTION. 3. PURPOSE; RULE OF CONSTRUCTION.

(a) Purpose. The purposes of this Act are—

    - to codify mobility and travel as civil liberties and to prohibit their conversion into conditional privileges;
    
    - to secure the rights of owners to maintain, repair, modify, and preserve lawfully owned vehicles and conveyances;
    
    - to prevent forced obsolescence, digital enclosure, and vendor lock-in in vehicle systems and toolchains;
    
    - to prohibit the use of transportation systems as instruments of mass surveillance, social scoring, or coercive control;
    
    - to impose enforceable duties on jurisdictions to maintain, modernize, and operate essential mobility infrastructure to durable, safe, and efficient standards;
    
    - to prohibit intentional throughput degradation (“traffic shaping”) and to require capacity solutions where persistent congestion exceeds defined thresholds; and
    
    - to require public transit systems to operate safely, orderly, and self-funded, with due process protections for riders and without functioning as predictable venues for criminal conduct.
    
(b) Rule of construction. This Act shall be construed—

    - to favor liberty over administrative convenience;
    
    - to require performance-based and risk-proportionate safety regulation; and
    
    - to prohibit pretextual regulation that has the primary purpose or practical effect of artificial scarcity, dependency, cost inflation, or confinement-by-design.
    
## SECTION. 4. DEFINITIONS.

In this Act:

(1) Conveyance. The term “conveyance” includes a vehicle, personal aircraft, and any emergent or advanced mobility device or platform used for travel or transport.

(2) Vehicle. The term “vehicle” includes any motor vehicle, motorcycle, off-road vehicle, trailer, and any software-defined or connected vehicle.

(3) Personal aircraft. The term “personal aircraft” includes ultralights, general aviation aircraft, eVTOL or equivalent aircraft, and other personal flight systems.

(4) Manufacturer. The term “manufacturer” means any person or entity that designs, assembles, imports, brands, distributes, or exercises control over a conveyance or any essential system thereof, including firmware, software, diagnostics, security authentication, telematics, parts pairing, or service toolchains.

(5) Owner. The term “owner” means a purchaser, lessee, or any lawful long-term possessor of a conveyance, including an agent acting with the owner’s authorization.

(6) Independent repair provider. The term “independent repair provider” includes any person or entity other than a manufacturer (or its controlled affiliates) who repairs or services conveyances, including an owner performing self-repair.

(7) Essential function. The term “essential function” means any function necessary for ordinary and safe use, legal compliance, repair, diagnostics, calibration, inspection, or continued operability of a conveyance.

(8) Telematics. The term “telematics” means any sensor, modem, module, service, or software that collects, transmits, stores, or processes conveyance data.

(9) Conveyance data. The term “conveyance data” means data generated by operation or status of a conveyance, including diagnostic data, performance data, and telematics data.

(10) Essential mobility infrastructure. The term “essential mobility infrastructure” includes public roads, bridges, tunnels, traffic-control systems, rail corridors, transit infrastructure, ports, and facilities necessary to ensure safe, nondiscriminatory travel, including rural access and freight movement.

(11) Traffic shaping. The term “traffic shaping” means intentional engineering or operational manipulation of transportation throughput—by signal timing, lane allocation, speed governance, chokepoints, road diets, or equivalent measures—implemented primarily to manufacture congestion, deter lawful travel, coerce mode shift, penalize geographic movement, or create mobility rationing, rather than to address demonstrable safety hazards through least-restrictive means.

(12) Utility Vehicle Class. The term “Utility Vehicle Class” means the class of vehicles defined under section 303 intended to preserve access to low-cost, mechanically simple, durable, repairable vehicles suitable for work and ordinary use.

## TITLE I — RIGHT TO TRAVEL; PROHIBITIONS ON MOBILITY COERCION AND SURVEILLANCE

## SECTION. 101. RIGHT TO TRAVEL; LIMITS ON ROUTINE CHECKPOINTS AND COMPULSORY IDENTIFICATION.

(a) Right. Every person lawfully within the jurisdiction possesses the right to travel freely within the territory.

(b) Prohibited practices. No government entity may impose—
    - internal passports;
    - routine checkpoints; or
    - compulsory identification demands,
    
except upon reasonable, articulable suspicion of specific unlawful conduct supported by objective facts, and subject to prompt judicial review upon challenge.

(c) No confinement-by-design. No transportation planning, zoning practice, pricing scheme, algorithmic scoring regime, or administrative barrier may operate with the primary purpose or practical effect of confining ordinary persons to prescribed geographic zones or penalizing lawful travel.

## SECTION. 102. PROHIBITION ON MOBILITY AS A SURVEILLANCE CHANNEL.

(a) General rule. Transportation systems shall not be used as instruments of mass surveillance, behavioral control, or social scoring.

(b) Limits on government collection. Mobility-related data collection by a government entity shall be—

    - minimal and purpose-limited;
    
    - time-limited and non-retentive beyond the minimum period required for the lawful purpose; and
    
    - subject to judicial authorization where individualized suspicion is required by law, with recorded findings.
    
(c) Remedies. Data obtained in violation of this section is inadmissible and without legal effect in any civil or criminal proceeding, and any use, retention, or dissemination of such data constitutes an actionable violation under Title VII.

TITLE II — OWNERSHIP, REPAIR, SOFTWARE CONTROL, AND DATA RIGHTS

## SECTION. 201. RIGHT TO OWN, OPERATE, MAINTAIN, MODIFY, AND REPAIR.

(a) Right. The right to travel includes the right to own, operate, maintain, modify, and repair lawfully owned conveyances.

(b) Non-discrimination against simplicity and durability. No government entity may prohibit or disfavor mechanically simple, durable, repairable, or long-lived designs absent a compelling and demonstrable safety necessity supported by evidence and implemented through the least liberty-restrictive means.

## SECTION. 202. AUTOMOTIVE RIGHT TO REPAIR; PARTS, TOOLS, DOCUMENTATION, AND DIAGNOSTICS.

(a) Access requirement. A manufacturer shall make available to owners and independent repair providers, on fair and nondiscriminatory terms and at reasonable cost—

    - replacement parts (including component-level parts where feasible);
    
    - special tools, service equipment, and calibration tools;
    
    - service and repair documentation;
    
    - diagnostic software and codes; and
    
    - security and immobilizer service pathways sufficient for lawful repair and restoration of function, subject to subsection (c).
    
(b) Prohibited restrictions. A manufacturer shall not use DRM, authentication locks, parts pairing, contractual terms, firmware restrictions, cloud dependencies, service gating, or other technological measures to prevent or materially impair lawful repair, maintenance, calibration, inspection, or modification.

(c) Security exception—owner-authorized pathway required. A manufacturer may implement security measures to prevent theft and fraud only if the manufacturer provides an owner-authorized method for independent service that preserves security without granting the manufacturer monopoly control.

## SECTION. 203. WARRANTY FAIRNESS.

(a) No blanket voiding. A manufacturer shall not void or deny warranty coverage solely because an owner or independent repair provider performed repair or maintenance using lawful parts and reasonable methods.

(b) Causation allowed. A manufacturer is not required to warrant damage caused by modifications that exceed design limits or materially degrade safety, reliability, or drivetrain integrity, provided the manufacturer bears the burden of showing causation by clear evidence.

## SECTION. 204. BAN ON FORCED OBSOLESCENCE, SUBSCRIPTION HOSTAGE PRACTICES, AND DIGITAL SABOTAGE.

(a) Prohibition. A manufacturer (or any controlling service provider) may not design, license, or enforce conveyance systems in a manner that—

    - strips meaningful ownership from lawful purchasers;
    
    - coerces perpetual payment for essential functions;
    
    - disables or materially impairs repairability or preservation; or
    
    - creates dependency without a reasonable and accessible exit.
    
(b) Updates and remote policy changes. A manufacturer may not materially degrade performance or essential function through updates, account actions, service termination, or remote policy changes, except to address a demonstrated safety defect through narrowly tailored measures with notice and an owner-accessible remedy.

(c) Mandatory disclosure. At point of sale, manufacturers and dealers shall disclose in plain language—

    - any feature requiring subscription to operate;
    
    - any feature that can be removed or reduced remotely; and
    
    - any essential function dependent on external services.
    
## SECTION. 205. CONVEYANCE DATA RIGHTS; TELEMATICS CONTROLS.

(a) Owner control. Owners retain control of conveyance data associated with their conveyances and use.

(b) Consent. Collection and use of conveyance data requires informed, revocable consent, except for minimal data strictly necessary to provide a service the owner affirmatively requests.

(c) Access and export. Owners have the right to access, export, and transmit conveyance data in a usable format to a chosen repair provider or third-party service.

(d) Interoperability interface. Manufacturers shall provide documented interfaces sufficient for owner-authorized interoperability.

(e) Non-retaliation. Manufacturers shall not retaliate against an owner for lawful data export or independent service by degrading features, performance, warranty administration, pricing, or service access.

## SECTION. 206. LIMITS ON REMOTE DISABLEMENT AND REMOTE CONTROL.

(a) Prohibition absent judicial order. No manufacturer, lender, insurer, platform, or government entity may remotely disable, immobilize, degrade, or seize control of a conveyance absent judicial authorization with documented scope and duration.

(b) No standing remote-control architecture for routine enforcement. A manufacturer shall not ship conveyances with a default architecture that enables standing remote control powers for generalized surveillance, routine enforcement, or administrative coercion.

## SECTION. 207. RIGHT TO REPLICATE DISCONTINUED PARTS AND COMPATIBLE COMPONENTS.

(a) Right. Upon discontinuation of production or support for an essential part, component, module, or toolchain by a manufacturer, an owner and an independent repair provider may lawfully replicate, reverse-engineer, or produce compatible parts and components for purposes of repair, maintenance, preservation, restoration, or continued operability.

(b) Safety compliance. Parts produced under subsection (a) shall meet applicable safety standards and shall not be represented as manufacturer-original unless true. Nothing in this section limits liability for unsafe workmanship, fraudulent representation, or willful defeat of safety-critical systems.

(c) Trademark limitation. Replication or production under this section shall not use a trademark, trade dress, or branding in a confusing manner, and shall not falsely imply manufacturer sponsorship or origin.

(d) Non-interference. A manufacturer shall not use software, firmware, authentication systems, parts pairing, contractual terms, or other technological measures to prohibit or materially impair the use of parts produced under subsection (a), except as necessary to address a demonstrated and material safety risk through narrowly tailored means with an owner-authorized alternative pathway.

##TITLE III — ANTI–PRICE ENGINEERING; UTILITY VEHICLE ACCESS; COMPARATIVE ACCESS STANDARD

## SECTION. 301. PROHIBITION OF ARTIFICIAL COST INFLATION AND REGULATORY CAPTURE IN VEHICLE STANDARDS.

(a) General rule. No government entity may use safety, environmental, efficiency, or administrative authority as a pretext to—

    - artificially inflate the cost of vehicle ownership;
    
    - mandate luxury features or proprietary technologies where simpler alternatives suffice;
    
    - require surveillance-dependent or network-dependent technologies where unnecessary; or
    
    - force premature obsolescence.
    
(b) Burden and findings. Any rule that materially increases average purchase price, repair cost, or complexity must be supported by written findings showing—

    - necessity;
    
    - proportionality; and
    
    - demonstrable safety benefit supported by evidence,
    
and must be the least restrictive alternative.

(c) Automatic sunset of high-cost rules. Any rule covered by subsection (b) shall sunset on a schedule set by the Legislature unless affirmatively renewed after capture review.

## SECTION. 302. COMPARATIVE ACCESS STANDARD; PRESERVATION OF AFFORDABLE, REPAIRABLE CLASSES.

(a) Standard. Residents shall retain access to affordable, reliable, mechanically intelligible vehicles comparable to those lawfully available to ordinary persons in other advanced nations and global peers, including work-grade utility platforms commonly available internationally.

(b) Justiciable claim. A person has a cause of action to challenge a regulatory scheme whose cumulative effect eliminates durable, simple, low-cost utility vehicles as a class.

## SECTION. 303. UTILITY VEHICLE CLASS SAFE HARBOR (THE “HILUX/WRANGLER STANDARD”).

(a) Establishment. Not later than 12 months after enactment, the Legislature shall define a Utility Vehicle Class to preserve practical access to durable, mechanically simple, repairable utility vehicles suitable for ordinary work and daily use (the “Hilux/Wrangler Standard” as shorthand for this policy objective).

(b) Performance-based compliance pathway. For the Utility Vehicle Class—

    - standards shall be performance-based and outcome-oriented, and shall not require proprietary technologies or vendor-controlled implementation pathways;
    
    - compliance pathways shall permit mechanically simple solutions and field-serviceable designs;
    
    - no rule may require network connectivity, subscriptions, account dependencies, or vendor-locked software as a condition of lawful ownership, registration, inspection, or ordinary operation, absent a compelling and demonstrable safety necessity under section 301; and
    
    - safety requirements shall prioritize robustness and repairability over disposable complexity.
    
(c) Anti-pretext construction. A government entity may not evade this section by adopting requirements that, in the aggregate, eliminate the Utility Vehicle Class in practice through cost, complexity, or dependence on controlled services.

## TITLE IV — PERSONAL FLIGHT AND EMERGENT CONVEYANCES

## SECTION. 401. RIGHT TO PERSONAL FLIGHT AND EMERGENT CONVEYANCES.

(a) Right. The right to travel includes the right to develop, own, and operate personal aircraft and emergent conveyances.

(b) Limits. Restrictions shall be performance-based and risk-proportionate. Safety standards shall not be used to prohibit entire classes of conveyance absent a clear showing of unavoidable and substantial danger.

(c) Anti-prohibition-by-burden. Administrative burdens shall not be imposed where cumulative burden effectively prohibits lawful use by an ordinarily competent individual.

## TITLE V — INFRASTRUCTURE DUTY; ROAD QUALITY; EFFICIENCY; CAPACITY; TRANSIT SAFETY AND SELF-FUNDING

## SECTION. 501. DUTY TO MAINTAIN, MODERNIZE, AND ENSURE DURABILITY OF ROADS.

(a) Duty. Each jurisdiction responsible for essential mobility infrastructure shall maintain and modernize such infrastructure to safe, durable, and efficient standards.

(b) Road quality standards and dashboards. Not later than 180 days after enactment, each responsible jurisdiction shall publish measurable standards and a public dashboard, including at minimum—

    - maximum allowable pothole density by road class;
    
    - maximum time-to-repair by severity tier;
    
    - resurfacing and rehabilitation cycles by pavement type; and
    
    - independent condition audits at regular intervals.
    
(c) Cure plans and intervention. Failure to meet standards for a sustained period triggers mandatory cure plans, deadlines, and court-supervised remediation under Title VII. Compliance with the duties and standards in this Title shall be a condition of eligibility for Federal highway, bridge, transit, or surface transportation funds under title 23, United States Code, or other applicable Federal transportation programs. The Secretary of Transportation shall withhold or condition apportionments for jurisdictions in sustained noncompliance, following notice and opportunity for cure.

## SECTION. 502. RIGHT TO EFFICIENCY; PROHIBITION ON TRAFFIC SHAPING.

(a) Prohibition. A jurisdiction may not implement traffic shaping.

(b) Safety exception (least restrictive means). A jurisdiction may adopt throughput-affecting measures only if the jurisdiction issues written findings supported by evidence that—

    - a demonstrable safety hazard exists;
    
    - the measure is narrowly tailored; and
    
    - no less liberty-restrictive alternative would address the hazard comparably.
    
(c) Efficiency entitlement; performance triggers. Residents have the right to demand reasonable throughput and travel-time reliability. The Legislature shall establish performance triggers, including at minimum—

    - corridor delay thresholds;
    
    - recurring congestion thresholds; and
    
    - travel-time reliability thresholds,
    
measured over twelve continuous months.

(d) Required response. When triggers are exceeded, a jurisdiction shall, on a defined timeline—

    - implement engineering fixes to reduce delay; or
    
    - expand capacity where feasible under section 503,
    
unless the jurisdiction proves physical impossibility or disproportionate harm and adopts an alternative achieving comparable throughput.

## SECTION. 503. DUTY TO BUILD NECESSARY BELTWAYS, BYPASSES, AND CONNECTORS.

(a) Capacity duty. Where population growth, freight needs, or recurring congestion exceeds thresholds established under section 502(c), jurisdictions shall plan, permit, and construct necessary bypasses, beltways, highway connectors, grade separations, or equivalent capacity solutions.

(b) No avoidance. A jurisdiction may not avoid necessary capacity projects for the primary purpose of coercing immobility, forcing mode shift, or manufacturing congestion.

(c) Alternatives. If a jurisdiction declines a capacity project, it bears the burden to show by written findings that—

    - the project is physically infeasible or would cause disproportionate harm; and
    
    - the alternative adopted will achieve comparable throughput and safety outcomes.
    
## SECTION. 504. PUBLIC TRANSIT SAFETY, ORDER, AND SELF-FUNDING.

(a) Orderly, family-safe environments. Public transit systems shall maintain orderly, family-safe environments and shall not operate as predictable venues for assault, intimidation, open drug use, fencing stolen goods, or other criminal acts in the presence of families and children. Transit operators shall adopt and enforce conduct rules, maintain trained security presence calibrated to ridership risk, and provide prompt removal of offenders.

(b) No facilitation of habitual criminal conduct. Transit systems shall not subsidize or facilitate habitual criminal conduct through inadequate security, inadequate enforcement, or policies that predictably allow repeat offenders to use transit as a platform for crime or flight from crime. Nothing in this subsection authorizes dragnet checks, generalized surveillance, or collective punishment.

(c) Fare integrity and self-funding. Public transit systems shall be self-funding through fares, service contracting, advertising, and other lawful revenue, except for narrowly tailored subsidies explicitly enacted for—

    - disability access; or
    
    - hardship riders,
    
with defined eligibility, periodic auditing, and anti-fraud controls.

(d) Proportionate enforcement and due process. Fare enforcement and conduct enforcement shall be proportionate and nonviolent, and any exclusion order or rider ban shall include—

    - notice of the reasons;
    
    - an appeal mechanism;
    
    - a clear path to reinstatement upon compliance; and
    
    - a prohibition on collective punishment or dragnet checks.
    
## TITLE VI — VEHICLE SOFTWARE CONTINUITY ESCROW; RELATIONSHIP TO THE REPO ACT (FALLBACK MODULE)

## SECTION. 601. VEHICLE SOFTWARE CONTINUITY ESCROW (FALLBACK).

(a) Purpose. This section prevents bricking, toolchain hostage tactics, and permanent enclosure of essential vehicle firmware, diagnostics, and software ecosystems.

(b) Trigger events. If a manufacturer—

    - discontinues support for an essential digital control plane;

    - terminates essential activation, authentication, or update services; or

    - sunsets a platform such that essential function cannot be maintained on fair terms,

the manufacturer shall comply with subsection (c).

(c) Deposit. The manufacturer shall deposit with a qualified escrow agent, in a form sufficient for reproducibility and continuity—

    - source code or the closest lawful equivalent for essential firmware/software;
    
    - build systems, dependencies, and documentation sufficient to reproduce binaries;
    
    - interface specifications necessary for diagnostics, repair, calibration, and security maintenance; and
    
    - test vectors or validation materials reasonably required to ensure safety and integrity.
    
(d) Release. The escrow agent shall release the deposit to a public custodian on a timeline and under conditions prescribed by the Legislature if—

    - the manufacturer fails to restore support on fair terms; and

    - continued enclosure creates public harm, safety risk, or forced obsolescence addressed by this section.
    
## SECTION. 602. RELATIONSHIP TO THE REPO ACT; GAP-FILLER STATUS.

(a) Superseding coverage. If the REPO Act is enacted and applies to relevant vehicle systems, software, or toolchains covered by this Title, compliance with the REPO Act shall satisfy the requirements of this Title to the extent of overlap.

(b) Gap-filler. This Title shall remain operative and enforceable only to the extent that REPO coverage is absent, delayed, narrowed, or otherwise insufficient to prevent bricking, toolchain hostage tactics, or forced obsolescence addressed by section 601.

(c) Other laws. If another Federal law enacted after the date of this Act governs vehicle software continuity, escrow, or related continuity obligations and directly conflicts with this Title, such other law shall control to the extent of the conflict. This Title shall otherwise provide supplementary protections and remain fully operative.

## TITLE VII — ENFORCEMENT; PRIVATE RIGHTS OF ACTION; REMEDIES

## SECTION. 701. STANDING.

Any person injured by a violation of this Act has standing to bring an action, including any person denied mobility rights; subjected to unlawful restriction, surveillance-by-mobility, repair obstruction, cost inflation, traffic shaping, chronic road neglect, or unsafe transit conditions as described in Title V.

## SECTION. 702. PRIVATE RIGHT OF ACTION; ATTORNEY FEES.

(a) Cause of action. A person may bring a civil action in a court of competent jurisdiction for declaratory and injunctive relief, damages, and other appropriate relief.

(b) Fee shifting. A prevailing plaintiff shall recover reasonable attorney fees and costs. A prevailing defendant may recover fees only upon a finding that the action was frivolous or brought in bad faith.

## SECTION. 703. REMEDIES; COURT-SUPERVISED REMEDIATION.

(a) Remedies. Remedies include—

    - declaratory relief;

    - temporary, preliminary, and permanent injunctive relief;

    - actual damages;

    - statutory damages as prescribed by the Legislature;

    - punitive damages upon a finding of willful misconduct; and

    - specific performance, including mandated publication of dashboards, cure plans, and deadlines.

(b) Infrastructure remediation. In cases of chronic noncompliance with Title V, the court may order court-supervised remediation until standards are met, including appointment of an independent monitor.

## SECTION. 704. NON-IMMUNITY.

No sovereign, qualified, or administrative immunity shall bar relief for violations of this Act. (This section does not create criminal liability; criminal liability, if any, shall be provided by separate law.)

## TITLE VIII — EFFECTIVE DATE; TRANSITION

## SECTION. 801. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act takes effect 180 days after enactment, except as otherwise provided.

## SECTION. 802. IMPLEMENTATION DEADLINES.

    - Road standards and dashboards under section 501(b) shall be published not later than 180 days after enactment.

    - The first independent condition audit under section 501(b)(4) shall occur not later than 12 months after enactment.

    - The Utility Vehicle Class under section 303 shall be defined not later than 12 months after enactment.

## SECTION. 803. SEVERABILITY.

If any provision of this Act, or the application thereof to any person or circumstance, is held invalid, the remainder of this Act, and the application of the remaining provisions, shall not be affected.

## SECTION. 804. PREEMPTION.

This Act preempts any State or local law, rule, or requirement that conflicts with or frustrates the purposes of this Act, including restrictions on vehicle repair access, data rights, remote disablement bans, utility vehicle classes, traffic-shaping prohibitions, or infrastructure maintenance duties, to the extent such State or local measures burden interstate commerce, interstate travel, or uniform national standards for vehicle ownership and mobility.
